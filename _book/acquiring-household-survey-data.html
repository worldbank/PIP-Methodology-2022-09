2 Acquiring household survey data

This section describes the data sources used for measuring poverty and inequality as well as how the data are selected and obtained.

2.1 Selection criteria and source of data

For developing countries, the Poverty and Inequality Platform relies on household surveys carried out by the countriesâ€™ National Statistical Offices (NSO). These surveys typically interview a random sample of households in the country, asking for details about their income and consumption patterns and much more.

For high-income countries in the European Union (with the exception of Germany), the data are obtained from the EU Statistics on Income and Living Conditions (EU-SILC). EU-SILC data are partly obtained from surveys and partly obtained from administrative records. For other high-income economies (Australia; Canada; Germany; Israel; Japan; South Korea; Taiwan, China; and the United States) data from the Luxembourg Income Study (LIS) Database are used. These data rely on surveys carried out by NSOs or by academic institutions. Data from the LIS database are also used for high-income countries from the European Union for years predating EU-SILC.

Occasionally, a survey filling the above criteria is not incorporated into the platform because the World Bank does not have access to the data, due to concerns with the survey design or welfare aggregate, or because the auxiliary data needed (such as consumer price indices and purchasing power parities) are unavailable. Whenever decisions are made to exclude a survey from the platform, they are done by the Global Poverty Working Group within the World Bank and the reason for the exclusion of a data point is summarized in the â€œWhatâ€™s Newâ€ documents.

2.2 Representativeness

In the majority of cases, the surveys used are representative of all households in a country. This means that they are based on a random selection of all households in the country, typically defined from the most recent census. Two factors prevent these surveys from being representative of the entire population:

  1. Some individuals do not reside in households and hence are not captured by the above frame. This particularly concerns homeless people and individuals residing in nursery homes, prisons, and displacement camps.
  2. At times, conflicts and extreme weather events prevent the enumerators from going to certain parts of the country. In these cases, some geographical areas may be excluded from the survey. The 2018-19 Nigeria Living Standards Survey, for example, is not representative of the Borno state, since parts of the state became inaccessible over the course of the survey.

For the purpose of calculating global or regional poverty, the poverty rates estimated in the surveys are assumed to be representative of the entire population despite of the two shortcomings above. This is largely because acquiring any alternative poverty estimate for the missing populations is challenging. In certain scenarios, the survey design allows the consumption or income of households not covered to be imputed, in which case the second problem is addressed.

The PIP also contains some surveys which explicitly only target the urban or rural part of the population. These surveys are indicated as [waiting to see how they will appear in PIP]. These surveys are not used for the purposes of calculating regional or global poverty, with only one exception. This exception concerns Argentina which has no nationally representative surveys, but only surveys representative of its urban population. For the urban population of Argentina, these surveys are used for the regional and global poverty counts, while the rural population of Argentina gets the same treatment as countries for which we have no data.

2.3 How data are obtained

Most of the data for East Asia & Pacific, the Middle East & North Africa, South Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa are obtained from countriesâ€™ National Statistical Offices. In many low-income countries, the Poverty and Equity Global Practice of the World Bank is cooperating with the National Statistical Offices and supporting their efforts to conduct household surveys and measure poverty. Data are obtained through these partnerships.

For Latin America and the Caribbean, most of the data are obtained from CEDLASâ€™s Socio-Economic Database for Latin America and the Caribbean (SEDLAC).

For most high-income countries data are obtained through the Luxembourg Income Study or EU-SILC.

All of the data are harmonized by the Poverty and Equity Global Practice of the World Bank and then passed on to the Poverty and Inequality Platform.

2.4 Grouped vs.Â unit record data

In some cases, countries are unwilling to share unit-record data with the World Bank but they are willing to share grouped data. Grouped data are aggregated data, often to 5,10, 20 or 100 quantiles of the distribution. A country might have data on, for example, the mean income of each percentile or the income share of each percentile. For the purposes of estimating poverty and inequality, a Lorenz curve is estimated from these grouped data upon which inequality and poverty can be estimated (the method behind this is described here). Grouped data are used for Algeria, China, Guyana, Suriname, Turkmenistan, Trinidad & Tobago, Venezuela, and the United Arab Emirates and more countries for earlier years. 2.5 Multiply imputed micro data

At times, unit record data from household surveys are not representative of all households since conflict or other safety concerns prohibit enumerators from getting to all areas of a country (see discussion here). In some of these cases, some household information was collected, either because part of the interviews were finished before a crisis unfolded, or because shorter interviews without a full welfare aggregate were conducted in an attempt to minimize safety risks. In these cases, a full welfare aggregate can be imputed using statistical models. In an attempt to minimize the uncertainty of such methods, described in greater detail in [South Sudan and Somalia papers], they often work by creating multiple imputed datasets, calculating statistics of interest in each of these dataset, and averaging over them to acquire a final estimate.
